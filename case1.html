Impact / Effort matrix

While working on this project I also read various texts aimed at helping UX design students with the design challenges they will be given in job interviews. For this project i chose to follow Artiom Dashinskyâ€™s recommendation of starting the exercise with a simple Impact / Effort analysis.

Survey

The Impact / Effort matrix noted that reaching high net worth individuals would potentially bring in more revenue to the cause, but as I was enrolled in college at the time and only had a few weeks to do the assignment, fellow students were my most readily available source for user insights. I therefore based the project on 20 - 30 year olds as the intended target audience.

I created a Google questionnaire with a view to capture their living situations, their news- and social media habits, their experiences with charitable giving and their attitudes to NGOs in general. With a view to get the tone right I took some cues from Sara Wachter-Boettchâ€™s book 'Technically Wrong', and added a final section of questions designed to get a feel for their experiences (and possible trauma) related to the pandemic.

Affinity Mapping

In a Miro board I copied the survey responses onto post it notes. I then used one of my favourite UX tools, affinity mapping, and relatively quickly discovered a number of broader trends and attitudes.

(I've blurred out the individual responses.)

Personas

In a separate workspace I copied the main affinity keywords from the mapping session onto a new set of post it notes. As I ordered and began to link these keywords together, three distinct attitudes began to emerge. These formed the cores of three personas who would go on to inform the subsequent work with the project.

By inserting a middle layer of affinity mapping between the survey response and the personas, I now had personas that were one step removed from the questionnaire respondents. These reflected broader patterns in the target population, rather than being mere charicatures of whoever had initially grabbed my attention during the survey phase. This way I could lower the risk of an uncious bias in favour of the respondents who most closely resembled a â€˜preferredâ€™ user profile.

Lo-fi Wireframes

In the pen and paper phase I was mindful of the target audienceâ€™s preference for mobile phones, so the layouts were sketched with a view to create a single, scrolling-friendly landing page. I divided the images and information into clearly defined and geometrically delineated sections that could easily be stacked and re-organised depending on the screen size.

Mid-fi Wireframes

The mid-fi wireframes were my first attempt at establishing a fundamental information architecture. But more importantly I used this as an opportunity to test three distinct aesthetic styles. Using Figma and Figma Mirror I sought responses to these in a series of user interviews with representatives of the target group.

Briefly explained, the first style attempted to represent the closest thematic link to the upbeat tone and saturated colours of social media, the medium through wich one would most likely promote the cause to the target audience. The second option was based on the warm rich tones often associated with an African aesthetic, without making it too obvious, and the strong typographic contrasts refelect the visual language of news media from where the target audience received their updates on the pandemic. The final (and least popular) style sought to reflect the fight against the pandemic as a medical and scientific endeavour.

Photos

For all three themes I used Creative Commons licensed material from African photographers. I chose motifs featuring subjects in the same age bracket as the target audience, in relatable poses and fashionable clothes, thus carefully avoiding the many overused visual clichÃ©s from Africa that one typically sees in NGO marketing material. Editing them into grayscale was a pragmatic decision, so as to bring visual cohesion to photos taken by photographers who may have used widely different camera equipment and editing techinques.

Hi-fi wireframes

Given the seriousness of the subject matter, my test users felt that the most sombre palette was the most appropriate for this project.

In the hi-fi wireframes I set out to sketch a mobile version, a tablet version as well as versions for laptop- and desktop screens.

WCAG

While somewhat stark, the high contrasts ensured that the palette delivered in terms of WCAG requirements.

Web animation

Much of the scrolling based dynamic on the page is made possible by creating CSS keyframe animations and then trigger them by various intersection observer functions written in Javascript.

Tracking and analysis

This project was my first chance to set up and implement various methods to get data once the site is up and running. I got visitor statistics with Google Analytics and heatmap recordings with Hotjar. A great way to continue to gather user data once the initial insight and testing phases are over.
