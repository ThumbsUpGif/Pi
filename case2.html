A college project where I had to come up with a new app idea for tourists visiting to Oslo, based entirely on needs I discovered through user interviews. The design presented here represents the third iteration of the project. As such, the timeframe between initial user resarch to present prototype is much longer than normal for such projects, but at the same time this means that the following presentation serves well in terms of showing my learning curve for the past 18 months.

I received the Oslo tourism app brief in August 2020, a time when Oslo was effectively closed to foreign tourists and access to sites of interest was significantly restricted due to pandemic guidelines.
This left me with two possible directions. I could base the concept on userâ€™s experiences before the pandemic, with a view to make an app that would become valid again a soon as restrictions lifted. Or I could confine my research to the challenges of present moment and hope that this extreme situation would create unique insights that could prove useful also when the pandemic restrictions lifted.

I chose the latter, i.e. to make it a â€˜domestic tourist during a pandemicâ€™ project, and set about interviewing friends, family and colleagues based in Oslo, asking them about their current urban leisure options. During the pandemic this was essentially confined to going for walks or to hang out in open spaces such as parks and public squares. (Oslo never had a full curfew). During this reseach I discovered several sources of frustration across different demographics, but most interestingly I found that parents of young children could spend quite a bit of time searching for suitable playgrounds.

Starting with a rather wide demographic I narrowed down my project by basing the app on the subgroup whose most strongly felt need was currently the least served by what's available.

The first two iterations were user tested with the Adobe XD app, but by the third iteration the Figma Mirror app had improved to the point where it could support impromptu user testing using a phone only.

When I arrived at the third iteration of this project I discareded all prevoius resarch and started anew by finding colour combinations using the Adobe Color tool. I then tested these with an incredibly useful heuristic I learned from the colours.cafe Instagram account where a palette is evaluated by applying it to generic vector images and also made into gradient swatches.
Finally, I used the Materialize color tool to explore more user friendly shades of the palette in question.

I learned that picking a colour palette for an app is a choice that far exceeds personal preferences. Taking a step away from the app UI and testing the palettes in a separate workspace proved useful.
Early iterations of the design system took inspiration from the playground itself, a site of energy and bold colours, but following user testing the design gradually changed to convey a greater degree of elegance and ease to use.
